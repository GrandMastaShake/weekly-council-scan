**Narrative:** The week answered the quarter's central question: was the 2026 inflation spike structural, or an energy shock? June CPI said shock. Headline fell 0.4% month-over-month — the largest one-month decline since April 2020 — dragging the year-over-year rate from 4.2% to 3.5%, and core was dead flat at 0.0% against a +0.2% consensus, the cleanest core print of the year. Shelter rose just 0.1% after a string of hot readings; gasoline plunged 9.7%. PPI confirmed twenty-four hours later: −0.3% headline against a flat forecast, pipeline pressure reversing as crude retraced its war premium. The bond market voted immediately — the 2-year dropped roughly 6 basis points on CPI and yields eased again on PPI — and the September rate-hike tail the market had been pricing (BofA had floated three hikes by December) effectively collapsed. The Conference Board's verdict: the June CPI "closes the door" on a July hike.

The equity tape, however, refused to celebrate. The S&P managed only +0.38% on both CPI and PPI days, then gave back −0.51% Thursday and −1.01% Friday to close the week at 7,457.69, down 1.55% — surrendering the 7,500 shelf it had defended for two weeks. The reason was rotation, not macro: the semiconductor complex entered a correction (−20% from highs), IBM posted its worst week in decades, and Netflix dropped 7% on guidance, while financials and energy absorbed the flows. Simultaneously, the Strait of Hormuz re-escalated — U.S. strikes on Iran, a reinstated naval blockade — sending oil up nearly 10% on the week and capping the disinflation relief. Cross-asset: DXY slid from ~101 toward 100.7, the 10-year stayed pinned at 4.58–4.61% (term premium and oil offsetting the dovish data), and VIX climbed from 15.0 to 18.5. The bond market said "disinflation." The stock market said "show me the earnings." Both are watching the same risk now: labor. NFP at +57K, jobs-hard-to-get at a five-year high, and the LEI rolling over again say the next regime fight is growth, not prices.
